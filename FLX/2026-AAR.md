# March 2026 FLX After-Action Review

## Cadre Perspective (CPT Wagner)

### Sustains

**Infantry Cadre Integration.** Having SSG Souza and SSG Goehrig embedded in the FLX was the single biggest value-add this iteration. They served as OPFOR, walked lanes alongside TACs, and provided a second tactical perspective grounded in running squad and platoon lanes full-time. Their feedback to candidates on formations, actions on the objective, and fire and maneuver carried weight because it came from people whose entire job is executing and evaluating these tasks. Continue requesting infantry cadre support for every FLX.

**FOB/COP Operations.** Using the facility as COP Seggel introduced real-world friction — ECP procedures, security sectors with dead space, interior clearing before occupation, and managing a defensive perimeter with limited personnel. The candidates had to think through problems that don't exist in a cold-start STX lane. The facility should remain a centerpiece of FLX design.

**Candidate Preparation.** Candidates did a decent job preparing for the FLX with the limited information provided ahead of time (Thursday training blocks on radios, ECP, defensive planning, etc.). The FLX preparation training model — candidates teach the skills they'll need to execute — is working as intended.

**Cadre Engagement.** Cadre across the board wanted to be out on the lanes walking with candidates, providing feedback, and running detailed AARs. The level of involvement was high and sustained throughout the weekend. That energy is what makes the FLX work.

**FLX OPORDs and Story Arc.** The operations orders were well-written after several rounds of refinement. The continuous narrative — recon finds the enemy, FRAGO sends a squad to clear them, ambush captures intel, capstone clears the cache — kept everyone oriented and invested in the scenario. Candidates and cadre understood why each mission was happening, which improved buy-in and planning quality.

**Tactical Execution Quality.** Assaulting objectives and actions on the objective were genuinely good across multiple iterations. Candidates demonstrated they can execute once they have a plan — the issue is getting them to produce that plan at the right level of complexity.

### Improves

**Training Intent Alignment — STX vs. Platoon Lanes.** The March FLX ran platoon-level operations from the start, which provided excellent training value but may have been too advanced too early. The primary training intent is preparing candidates for Phase 3 STX lanes, which are squad-level. We went above and beyond by running platoon lanes across the board, but candidates need to first master TLPs at the squad level before layering on platoon-level complexity (PL/PSG planning, multi-squad coordination, progressive scaffolding). Future FLX iterations should sequence squad-level lanes first to build the foundation, then introduce platoon-level operations once candidates demonstrate competence at the lower echelon.

**Sequencing Recommendation for April.** Start with squad-level lanes that mirror Phase 3 conditions — SL receives a mission, conducts TLPs, briefs the squad, and executes. Once candidates show they can handle that cycle, transition to platoon-level operations where the PL/PSG layer adds complexity. This matches the ability-grouping approach already discussed for April (TLP-focused group vs. tactical execution group).

### Looking Ahead

- Continue requesting infantry cadre for all FLX iterations — their tactical expertise and OPFOR capability are force multipliers
- Drive the story arc further in future iterations — refine the narrative thread and make it tighter
- Sequence training to match the phase progression: squad lanes first, platoon lanes after candidates demonstrate squad-level competence
- Explore airsoft for force-on-force (pricing out with CPT K)

### Key FTX Feedback Themes

**Squad Rotation Issues**:    

- Same squads remained in security positions throughout multiple iterations, limiting learning opportunities for some candidates
- Second squad stayed as assault squad across missions after initial planning decisions
- Third squad maintained security role throughout
- Recommendation: Rotate squad assignments between missions or renumber squads to change roles while maintaining taskings

**OPFOR Concerns**:     

- Some candidates felt they missed training value by serving as OPFOR rather than participating in planning/briefing
- Tradeoff identified: OPFOR sees tactical execution but misses TLP process and peer briefings
- Consensus that hearing op order briefs and participating in planning has more value for Phase 3 success than observing execution
- Phase 3 focus is on planning/briefing, not execution (execution is 5-10% of evaluation)

**Briefing Quality**:   

- Op order briefings during FTX were difficult to follow in some cases
- Vision was for squad leader-level briefing practice similar to Phase 3
- Execution didn't match intended standard due to resource constraints and planning decisions

**Leadership Rotation Success**:   

- Unplanned leadership rotation worked well - candidates progressed through roles (Joe → Team Leader → Squad Leader → PSG → PL)
- Allowed candidates to observe leaders above them, then step into those roles
- Previous leaders were removed from planning area to prevent over-influencing next leader
- Should be intentionally planned in future iterations

**Work/Rest Plan Implementation**:   

- Leadership did well managing rest plan when returning from missions
- Issue: Assault teams sometimes pulled tower security immediately after missions without adequate rest
- Need to better incorporate which squads were "heaviest worker" in previous mission when assigning next tasks

### Phase 3 Preparation Guidance

**What Resources to Request**:   

- Artillery or mortar support
- Grenades
- Colored smoke
- Notional security
- Satellite imagery
- Map (always ask)
- Depends on what instructor has available - if you see equipment on their kit, request it
- Must provide good justification for requests

**Critical Briefing Requirements**:   

- Must orient people to the map correctly
- Sand table must be positioned facing north (north arrow must align with actual north)
- Failure to orient sand table correctly can result in automatic failure
- Hit all main briefing criteria points - some carry more weight than others

**Philosophy on Tactics**:   

- Phase 3 does not evaluate tactical soundness - focus is on planning process
- Could theoretically use "Army Revolution style line formation" and pass if properly planned
- Most candidates are not going infantry - will learn branch-specific tactics at BOLC
- Critical thinking and decisiveness matter more than perfect tactical execution

### Cultural Expectations

**Feedback Environment**:    

- Professional discussion with no rank-pulling
- Everyone needs thick skin to accept constructive feedback
- Applies to cadre as well - must be able to receive feedback
- Be specific and reference what actually happened
- "I don't know" and "we didn't think about that" are valid answers
- Not trick questions - feedback directly shapes next class training
- If unable to take feedback, wrong profession

**Critical Thinking Emphasis**:    

- Course is about developing critical thinking, not just following orders
- Officers must think critically beyond what NCOs provide
- Goal is decisive leaders who will make decisions and learn from both success and failure
- Candidates encouraged to make requests - worst answer is "no"
- Should question confusing guidance and ask for clarification

**Learning from Failure**: 

- Failing is a form of success - shows what needs improvement
- Focus less on getting everything right, more on learning and adapting
- Program aims to produce decisive leaders, not perfect soldiers
