Terrain and Weather
Obstacles
Existing Obstacles:
Natural obstacles involve terrain, topography, and/or physical characteristics that affect or impede movement
Man-made obstacles involve human influence to affect or impede maneuver or arrangement
Reinforcing Obstacles:
Tactical Obstacles: Those obstacles employed to disrupt enemy formations, to turn them into a desired area, to fix them in position under direct and indirect fires, or to block enemy penetrations.
Protective Obstacles: Obstacles employed to assist a unit in its local, close-in protection. 

Terrain and Weather
Obstacles
 The terrain itself can actually be an obstacle.  This is indicated by classifying the terrain.
Classification of Terrain
Unrestricted: Indicates terrain free of constraints to movement. Nothing needs to be done to enhance mobility.
Restricted: Hinders movement to some degree. Little effort is needed to enhance mobility but units are unable to move at desired speeds or combat formations.
Severely Restricted: Hinders or slows movement in combat formations unless some effort is made to enhance mobility (i.e. Engineer Assets).

Observation and Fields of Fire
Observation  is the ability to see the threat either visually or through use of surveillance devices (NVG, thermals, etc).

Fields of Fire  are areas that a weapon or group of weapons may effectively cover with fire from a given position.

When talking about observation and fields of fire, talk about what you can see and engage, and what the enemy can see and engage.  How will limited visibility effect your observation?  Will weather like early morning fog effect your observation?  


Terrain and Weather
Cover and Concealment
Cover is protection from the effects of direct and indirect fires.

Concealment is protection from observation.

Armor, protective obstacles, buildings, trees, trenches, etc. will all have an effect on cover for you and the enemy.  Don‚Äôt forget about concealment from aerial observation as well as ground observation.
Highlight critical areas and what types of cover and quality are available (what size rounds will they provide cover from)
Highlight areas lacking cover or areas with only concealment

Upwind force generally has better visibility
Speed and direction can have significant impact on smoke and NBC operations
Wind carries noise and scent

Winds will be canalized in urban environments

Terrain and Weather
Precipitation
Heavy rains will effect visibility and can effect level of activity of all forces.
Rain and snow can combine with chemicals to create ‚Äúhot spots‚Äù in low-lying areas.
Mist can distribute chemicals more evenly and create high vapor concentrations
Can have significant effect on road washout and trafficability
Snow will reduce effectiveness of indirect fires, mines, and grenades by reducing the velocity of shrapnel
Rain will reduce the overall effectiveness of NVGs and thermals
Focus on visibility, sound, movement.

Terrain and Weather
Cloud Cover
Altitude of cloud cover influences aviation operations
Can reduce overall illumination and visibility
Can effect laser guided munitions (copperhead, hellfire, etc)
Greater than 70% cloud cover creates neutral gradient conditions which favor smoke and chemical munitions
Cloud cover can reduce temperature by blocking the sun, but can also increase temperatures during the night by trapping heat
Focus on illumination at night and that it can affect air.

Terrain and Weather
Temperature and Humidity
Hot and cold temperatures will effect personnel and equipment performance.  You must train in all conditions (try magazine changes and weapon operation in extreme cold).
Humidity (air density) will effect smoke operations and aircraft payloads
Hot weather may cause increased stress and frustration for enemy forces, friendly forces, and civilians (civil disorder) and can also significantly reduce activity
Additional risk mitigation must be in place for extreme hot and cold weather

COA Development
The purpose of COA DEV is to determine one or multiple ways to accomplish the mission.

A COA describes how the unit will generate overwhelming combat power against the enemy at the decisive point while mitigating risks/casualties.

Start planning from the decisive point and work back toward the start of the mission.


COA Development
Decisive Operation:  Those that directly accomplish the task of the higher headquarters.  Decisive operations conclusively determine the outcome of major operations, battles, and engagements.

Decisive Point:  A geographic place, specific key event, critical factor, or function that, when acted upon allows commanders to gain a marked advantage over an adversary or contribute materially to achieving success. 

Endstate:  Conditions that, when achieved, accomplish the mission.                                                                                                   


COA Development
Decisive Point
A geographic place:  (TERRAIN BASED) Focused on the seizure, retention, control, occupation, or denial of a specified piece of terrain that greatly influences the outcome.

A Key Event:  (TIME BASED) Focused on causing events to occur at or within a specific time that greatly influences the outcome.

An Enabling System:  (ENEMY BASED) Focused on the destruction, incapacitation, seizure, attrition, clearance, or neutralization of an enemy force, unit, capability that greatly influences the outcome.  

Your decisive point will usually accomplish one of these three.
