STANDARD

Officer Candidates will analyze missions, develop plans, and issue orders using the 8-step TLP process, demonstrating tactical and leadership competence as evaluated on the FLER

Evaluation: Field Leadership Evaluation Report (FLER) during STX lanes

WHY TLPs MATTER

TLPs are a dynamic process used by small-unit leaders to analyze a mission, develop a plan, and prepare for an operation.

TLPs enable leaders to:

  • Maximize available planning time
  • Develop effective, executable plans
  • Prepare their units for success
  • Adapt when the situation changes

"OCS stresses the use of TLP to effectively accomplish the mission." — OCSOP

THE BOTTOM LINE

TLPs are how you translate a commander's intent into action on the ground.

Your ability to execute TLPs determines whether your unit succeeds or fails.

METT-TC(I): THE MISSION VARIABLES

Mission variables describe the characteristics of the operational environment. Your analysis of METT-TC(I) drives every decision you make.

M - MISSION

Task and purpose from higher; specified, implied, essential tasks

E - ENEMY

Disposition, composition, strength, capabilities, COAs

T - TERRAIN & WEATHER

OAKOC analysis; visibility, precipitation, temperature effects

T - TROOPS AVAILABLE

Personnel, equipment, capabilities, training, strengths/weaknesses

T - TIME AVAILABLE

Higher's timeline, your timeline, enemy timeline, 1/3-2/3 rule

C - CIVIL CONSIDERATIONS

ASCOPE: Areas, Structures, Capabilities, Organizations, People, Events

(I) - INFORMATIONAL CONSIDERATIONS

Information environment impacts: cyber, media, PSYOP, OPSEC. How does information affect the fight?

NOTE: METT-TC(I) analysis is continuous. ATP 3-21.8 (2024) adds the "(I)" to emphasize that information operations affect every level of war—even at squad/platoon level, consider OPSEC, communications security, and the impact of visual documentation on your mission.

ATP 3-21.8 (2024); ATP 5-0.1; FM 6-0, Chapter 9

QUICK CHECK: METT-TC ANALYSIS

▶ If you have plenty of forces but limited time, what is your main constraint?

ANSWER: Time Available (the T in METT-TC)

This constraint shapes EVERYTHING. If higher needs the mission completed in 3 hours and you need 6, you either find a way to compress planning/execution or you have a problem. This drives your 1/3-2/3 rule: 1/3 for planning, 2/3 for execution.

▶ You spot a heavily armed enemy position on a ridgeline. Which METT-TC variable just changed?

ANSWER: Two actually changed: ENEMY (new enemy position/capability) and TERRAIN (the ridgeline affects your plan)

This is why recon is critical Step 5. A visual confirmation can alter your entire plan. If your analysis said "light enemy opposition" but recon shows "heavy fortified position," you need to reassess COAs immediately.

▶ What should you consider when analyzing TROOPS AND SUPPORT AVAILABLE?

ANSWER: Your organic and attached forces, their capabilities, equipment status, training level, fatigue, and morale. Also supporting assets (fires, aviation, engineers).

Know your Task Organization cold—who you have, what they can do, and what support is available to you.

MISSION ANALYSIS

UNDERSTAND THE MISSION (1 & 2 LEVELS UP)

Higher HQ Mission, Intent, Concept

Know what your commander and their commander want accomplished

COMMANDER'S INTENT

A clear, concise statement of what the unit must do to succeed with respect to terrain, enemy, and desired end-state.

PLs and SLs typically issue KEY TASKS rather than a formal commander's intent.

Constraints from Higher

Look for limits on your freedom of action—things you must do or cannot do. These shape your planning options.

MISSION STATEMENT FORMAT

WHO • WHAT • WHERE • WHEN • WHY

EXAMPLE:

[WHO] 1st Squad [WHAT] attacks to destroy enemy forces [WHERE] vic OBJ Silver [WHEN] NLT 100500JUL [WHY] to allow 2nd PLT to seize OBJ Gold.

WHY matters: Purpose allows you to adapt when conditions change.

AT OCS: As SL, "two levels up" = CO OPORD. As PL, it's BN OPORD.

ATP 5-0.1; FM 6-0

TASK TYPES: SPECIFIED, IMPLIED, ESSENTIAL

SPECIFIED TASKS

Explicitly stated in the order, overlays, or annexes.

"Secure LZ Helo" • "Search all structures" • "Conduct reconnaissance"

IMPLIED TASKS

Not stated but required to accomplish specified tasks. NOT SOP or routine tasks.

"Bring LZ marking kit" (from Secure LZ) • "Get graphics from CO" (from Build terrain model)

ESSENTIAL TASKS

Must be accomplished to achieve mission success. This becomes your mission statement task.

The task that accomplishes your purpose—what you MUST do.

OCS EXAMPLE:

SPECIFIED: PT formation 0530, ruck 4 mi IAW SOP

IMPLIED: Hygiene, hydrate, pack required weight, lay out PT/ruck uniform night prior, 15 min prior

ESSENTIAL: Formed NLT 0530, correct weight/uniform, ready to step

TACTICAL EXAMPLE:

SPECIFIED: 1st SQD raids OBJ FLUNKER NLT 150600JAN

IMPLIED: Build terrain model, confirm grid/route, ammo redistribution, LZ/ORP selection, PCC/PCI, comms check

ESSENTIAL: Raid destroys enemy CP at OBJ FLUNKER by 150600JAN (mission task)

KEY: Find all specified tasks in the order. Derive implied tasks. Identify which tasks are essential to mission success.

FM 6-0; ATP 5-0.1

PRACTICAL EXERCISE: TASK & PURPOSE MATCH

SCENARIO:

TASK: 1st Squad attacks to seize the bridge at grid 1234 5678 NLT 0600.

The task tells you WHAT to do. Now find the WHY.

QUESTION: Which PURPOSE matches that task?

✓ Correct. Seizing the bridge enables the company to cross—this explains WHY you're doing the task.
✗ This doesn't connect to the bridge seizure. Destroying enemy might happen, but it's not WHY you're seizing the bridge.
✗ Early warning is a recon/security purpose, not related to seizing a bridge.

Task = WHAT you do; Purpose = WHY you do it (how it helps the higher unit). →

THE NESTING CONCEPT

The "WHY" (purpose) is always nested vertically and horizontally.

VERTICAL NESTING (Up/Down Chain)

The Decisive Operation at each echelon has the same purpose as the higher unit.

BN DO purpose = CO DO purpose = PLT DO purpose = SQD DO purpose

HORIZONTAL NESTING (Across Units)

Shaping Operation purposes support the Decisive Operation.

SO1: "Prevent enemy maneuver on DO" • SO2: "Provide freedom of maneuver for DO"

EXAMPLE NESTING:

BN DO: Destroy vic OBJ Bull

P: Facilitate MVT of TF along I-185

CO DO (A): Destroy vic OBJ Bull

P: Facilitate MVT of TF along I-185

CO SO (B): Fix vic OBJ Cow

P: Prevent enemy maneuver on DO

CO SO (C): Block vic OBJ Horn

P: Prevent enemy envelopment of DO

YOUR PURPOSE: Located in the Concept of Operation in your immediate higher HQ OPORD.

PRACTICAL EXERCISE: NESTING TASKS AND PURPOSES

SCENARIO:

BN DO: DESTROY enemy vic OBJ TIGER to SECURE MSR GRIZZLY for BDE. Your Company is the BN DO, your Platoon is the CO DO. You're attacking OBJ TIGER.

Q1: What should be your platoon's PURPOSE?

✓ Correct. As the DO, your purpose nests vertically—same as Battalion's purpose.
✗ Too vague—doesn't nest with higher purpose.
✗ That's your TASK, not PURPOSE.

Q2: 2nd Squad (Shaping Op) will FIX enemy on eastern flank. Their PURPOSE?

✓ Correct. Shaping Ops nest horizontally—they support the DO.
✗ That's the DO purpose. Shaping Ops support the DO differently.
✗ Task is FIX, not destroy. And this doesn't explain WHY.

TERRAIN ANALYSIS: OAKOC

O - OBSERVATION & FIELDS OF FIRE

Where can we see/shoot? Where can the enemy see/shoot?

A - AVENUES OF APPROACH

Routes to the objective. Consider friendly and enemy movement.

K - KEY TERRAIN

Terrain giving marked advantage to whoever controls it.

O - OBSTACLES

Natural and man-made. What blocks movement?

C - COVER & CONCEALMENT

Cover protects from fire. Concealment hides from observation.

WEATHER EFFECTS (CPTVW)

Cloud cover, Precipitation, Temperature, Visibility, Wind

AO vs AI

AO: What you own | AI: What can affect you

OCS: Terrain model shows OAKOC—use yarn for AAs, mark key terrain, show cover/concealment.

ATP 2-01.3; FM 3-90-1

WEATHER ANALYSIS: CPTVW

Analyze how weather affects BOTH sides.

Define the weather → Effects on enemy → Effects on friendly → Deductions ("so what")

C — CLOUD COVER

Affects CAS/aviation ops, illumination at night, laser-guided munitions. >70% cloud cover favors smoke operations.

P — PRECIPITATION

Rain/snow affect visibility, trafficability, road washout. Reduces NVG and thermal effectiveness. Snow reduces shrapnel velocity.

T — TEMPERATURE / HUMIDITY

Extreme temps affect personnel and equipment. Heat may reduce activity. Humidity affects smoke ops and aircraft payload.

V — VISIBILITY

Low visibility (<3km) limits aircraft and aerial sensors. Canalizes forces onto defined AAs. Consider BMNT, SR, SS, EENT.

W — WIND

Upwind force has better visibility. Affects smoke and NBC ops. Wind carries noise and scent. Heavy winds can mask movement noise.

THE "SO WHAT":

For each weather factor, determine:

  • How does this affect the enemy?
  • How does this affect me?
  • What will I do about it?

SAND TABLES & TERRAIN MODELS

A sand table or terrain model is essential for planning and order delivery.

It provides a three-dimensional visualization that clarifies terrain effects, movement, and tactical positioning.

WHEN TO BUILD/USE:

1. During TLP Step 3 (Make a Tentative Plan) to visualize options

2. When issuing the OPORD (Step 6) to brief the plan

3. During rehearsals (Step 7) to practice movement and actions

4. Anytime the situation changes and requires a new plan or adjustment

WHAT IT SHOWS:

  • Terrain slopes, ridges, and depressions
  • Movement routes and covered approaches
  • Key terrain and objectives
  • Enemy positions and friendly dispositions
  • Fire support locations and fields of fire

DOCTRINE NOTE: ATP 3-21.8 (2024) and FM 6-0 emphasize that visual aids—including sand tables, overlays, and terrain boards—are critical to reduce misunderstanding and increase subordinate confidence.

PRO TIP: Don't just talk at a map. Stand on or beside the model while you brief. It shows confidence and keeps subordinates engaged.

TERRAIN ANALYSIS WORKSHEET

The most important part of terrain analysis is the DEDUCTIONS — the "so what."

Anyone can define terrain. Leaders determine what it means to the fight.

4-COLUMN WORKSHEET APPROACH:

1. DEFINE

2. ENEMY

3. FRIENDLY

4. DEDUCTIONS

Define feature → Effects on enemy → Effects on friendly → "So what?" (Action)

EXAMPLE 1:

Define: River running N/S through AO

Enemy: Can use boats to move within AO

Friendly: No boats; river is obstacle

→ DEDUCTION: Place OP along water to observe/disrupt enemy movement

EXAMPLE 2:

Define: Dense vegetation near creeks

Enemy: Limits observation of our mvmt

Friendly: Slows movement; provides concealment

→ DEDUCTION: Use for covered approach; allocate extra mvmt time

TIP: The DEDUCTION is what matters. It's your decision based on analysis—the "so what" that goes into your COA.

DECISIVE POINT: THE KEY TO YOUR PLAN

A geographic place, key event, or enabling system that, when acted upon, provides a MARKED ADVANTAGE.

THREE TYPES OF DECISIVE POINTS:

TERRAIN-BASED

Seizure, retention, or control of key terrain that greatly influences the outcome.

"Seizure of Hill 425 is decisive because it provides overwatch of the enemy MSR."

TIME-BASED

Causing events to occur at or within a specific time that greatly influences the outcome.

"Crossing LD NLT 0430 is decisive because it enables attack during BMNT."

ENEMY-BASED

Destruction or neutralization of an enemy capability that greatly influences the outcome.

"Destruction of the enemy MG position is decisive because it eliminates his primary defense."

HOW THE DP WORKS:

BEFORE DP: Setting conditions, shaping

AT DP: The moment the scale tips in your favor

AFTER DP: Exploit success, consolidate

COA STATEMENT: "Decisive to this operation is [DP]. This is decisive because [why it provides marked advantage]."

TIP: Start your planning from the decisive point and work backwards, then forward to mission complete.

CHECK YOUR UNDERSTANDING: COA DEVELOPMENT

▶ QUESTION 1: At squad level, how many COAs do you usually develop?

ANSWER: Usually ONE COA because of time. Build the best single COA you can, then war-gaming it hard against MPCOA and MDCOA.

TLPs are time-crunched. Pick the strongest COA quickly and improve it through war-gaming instead of trying to compare multiple COAs.

▶ QUESTION 2: What is your Decisive Operation (DO) at squad level?

ANSWER: The action that MUST succeed to win. Everything else (shaping, security, support) exists to make the DO successful.

Example: "1st Fire Team assaults and clears OBJ Red" is the DO. Support-by-fire, security, and obscuration are shaping operations.

▶ QUESTION 3: What makes a mission statement complete?

ANSWER: It answers WHO, WHAT (essential task), WHEN, WHERE, and WHY (purpose).

If any of those are missing—especially the WHY—your squad won't fully understand what success looks like.

COA STATEMENT TEMPLATE

USE THIS FORMAT:

"The purpose of this operation is [YOUR UNIT'S PURPOSE].

Decisive to this operation is [DECISIVE POINT]. This is decisive because [WHY it provides marked advantage].

We will accomplish this by conducting [FORM OF MANEUVER].

We will assume tactical risk by [RISK].

We will mitigate this risk by [MITIGATION]."

END STATE (What does success look like?):

  • Friendly: Unit postured, prepared for next mission
  • Enemy: Destroyed, unable to affect our operations
  • Terrain: Key terrain secured, obstacles reduced
  • Civilian: Non-combatants protected (if applicable)

EXAMPLE:

"The purpose of this operation is to allow unhindered passage of the BN DO to OBJ HAMMER.

Decisive is seizure of OBJ SAW East. This is decisive because it prevents direct/indirect fires on the BN DO.

We accomplish this by conducting an envelopment.

We assume risk by moving across open terrain. We mitigate with IDF suppression and obscuration."

WAR-GAMING YOUR PLAN

PURPOSE: Find weaknesses in your plan BEFORE the enemy does.

THE WAR-GAME SEQUENCE:

ACTION → REACTION → COUNTERACTION

ACTION: What do YOU do? (Friendly action)

REACTION: What does the ENEMY do? (Enemy response)

COUNTERACTION: What do YOU do next? (Your response to enemy)

WAR-GAME AGAINST ENEMY COAs:

MPCOA: Your plan should defeat the enemy's most probable course of action.

MDCOA: Have contingencies for the enemy's most dangerous course of action.

WAR-GAME METHODS:

Box: Analyze each area/phase separately

Belt: Analyze across the entire frontage

Avenue-in-Depth: Follow each avenue from start to finish

WHAT WAR-GAMING REVEALS:

  • Gaps in your plan
  • Decision points
  • Friction points
  • Need for branches/sequels
  • Synchronization issues

AT SQUAD LEVEL: War-game your single COA against the MPCOA. Adjust your plan based on what you find.

FM 6-0; ATP 5-0.1

SCENARIO: WAR-GAME YOUR PLAN

YOUR PLAN:

1st Squad assaults OBJ Green via northern approach (DO). 2nd Squad provides suppressive fire from the ridge to the west (SO). You expect light enemy resistance based on HUMINT.

YOUR ACTION:

1st Squad crosses LD at 0630 and moves toward OBJ. After 200 meters, they reach the draw...

ENEMY REACTION (MPCOA):

Enemy opens fire from OBJ with MG position you didn't detect. 1st Squad takes 50% casualties in first 30 seconds. 2nd Squad's suppressive fire from the ridge is ineffective—wrong angle.

QUESTION: What did your war-gaming miss?

✗ Artillery wasn't part of your TLP scope. This is a squad-level operation.
✓ CORRECT! Your intelligence said "light resistance" but you should have asked: "What if they're stronger?" War-gaming against MDCOA would have revealed your southern approach vulnerability and SO position weakness.
✗ Speed in the draw would just get you killed faster. The issue is lack of planning against the MDCOA.

This is why war-gaming against BOTH MPCOA and MDCOA is critical. →

FM 6-0; ATP 5-0.1

BRANCHES AND SEQUELS: CONTINGENCY PLANNING

War-gaming reveals decision points. Branches and sequels are your "if-then" plans for those points.

BRANCH (Contingency)

An "if-then" plan for a significant change during execution.

"IF we receive contact before reaching the ORP, THEN we will..."

"IF the enemy reinforces from the north, THEN we will..."

SEQUEL (Follow-on)

The plan for what happens after the current operation—success or failure.

"After securing OBJ, we will consolidate and prepare for exfil to..."

"If we fail to achieve surprise, we will break contact and move to AA..."

SQUAD-LEVEL EXAMPLES:

Branch: "If support-by-fire team takes casualties, Alpha Team shifts to support."

Branch: "If primary route is blocked, we use alternate route via creek bed."

Sequel: "After raid, move to rally point and prepare for CASEVAC."

WHY THIS MATTERS:

  • Shows tactical thinking beyond the DP
  • Prepares squad for friction
  • Reduces hesitation in contact
  • Enables subordinate initiative

AT OCS: Brief at least one branch ("if-then" contingency) in your OPORD. It demonstrates you thought beyond the base plan.

OPORD PARAGRAPH 3: EXECUTION

Para 3 is where you explain HOW you will accomplish the mission.

This is the heart of your order—spend time here.

a. KEY TASKS (Commander's Intent at PLT/SQD)

What success looks like. At squad level, list key tasks that must happen for mission success.

b. CONCEPT OF THE OPERATION

Decisive Point: What is decisive and why

Form of Maneuver: Envelopment, flank attack, etc.

Scheme of Maneuver: How we get from LD to OBJ (phases)

End State: What conditions define success

c. TASKS TO SUBORDINATE UNITS

DO: 1st Team attacks to destroy... in order to...
SO1: 2nd Team supports by fire... in order to...
SO2: 3rd Team provides security... in order to...

d. COORDINATING INSTRUCTIONS

Timeline: WARNORD, OPORD, rehearsal, SP, LD times

PIR: Priority Intelligence Requirements

ROE: Rules of Engagement, escalation of force

Signals: Lift/shift fire, phase line crossings

Contingencies: Branches (if-then plans)

FIRES (if applicable):

Fire support tasks, targets, triggers, and observers.

At OCS, know where fires fit even if you're not calling for them.

COMMON FAILURES:

  • Tasks without purposes
  • No decisive point stated

FM 6-0; ATP 3-21.8

FIRE SUPPORT INTEGRATION

Even at squad/platoon level, you should understand how to request and integrate indirect fires.

Fires are a combat multiplier—know how they fit in your plan.

CALL FOR FIRE FORMAT:

1. OBSERVER ID: "Bravo 2-1, this is Red 1..."

2. WARNING ORDER: "Fire mission, adjust/fire for effect"

3. TARGET LOCATION: Grid, polar, or shift from known point

4. TARGET DESCRIPTION: What you're shooting at

5. METHOD OF ENGAGEMENT: Type of adjustment, danger close

6. METHOD OF FIRE & CONTROL: When to fire

EXAMPLE:

"Steel 1, this is Red 1. Fire mission, adjust fire. Grid 12S AB 12345 67890. Enemy squad in the open. Request HE, VT. Fire when ready."

WHERE FIRES FIT IN THE OPORD:

Para 1: Enemy positions to target

Para 3: Fire support tasks, targets, triggers

Concept: How fires support maneuver

Coord Instructions: Lift/shift signals

FIRE SUPPORT TASKS:

  • Suppress: Reduce effectiveness
  • Neutralize: Render ineffective
  • Destroy: Physically eliminate
  • Obscure: Smoke to mask movement
  • Illuminate: Light up the battlefield

AT OCS:

You won't always call fires, but know how to integrate them in your plan. Include targets, triggers, and observer responsibilities in your OPORD.

LEADERSHIP PRESENCE IN ORDER DELIVERY

Poor leadership presence during order delivery is a common cause of "N" ratings on the FLER.

WHAT WE'RE LOOKING FOR:

Command Presence

  • Speak with confidence and authority
  • Make eye contact with subordinates
  • Project your voice—don't mumble
  • Stand upright, don't slouch over notes

Technical Proficiency

  • Know your plan—don't just read
  • Use the terrain model effectively
  • Answer questions with competence

THE BOTTOM LINE

Your subordinates need to trust that you know what you're doing. If you don't look and sound like a leader, they won't follow you with confidence.

COMMON MISTAKES:

  • Reading verbatim from notes
  • Speaking too quietly
  • Failing to use terrain model
  • Unable to answer basic questions
  • Skipping confirmation brief

REHEARSAL TYPES

Rehearsals ensure everyone understands the plan and their role in it.

The type you use depends on time available and what you need to validate.

Subordinates brief their understanding of the order. Used immediately after OPORD.

"1st Team, brief your task, purpose, and timeline."

BACKBRIEF

Subordinate leaders present their plan to you. Validates their understanding and their solution.

Higher uses this to verify subordinate planning is synchronized.

COMBINED ARMS REHEARSAL

Integrates all elements—maneuver, fires, sustainment. Walk-through on terrain model or actual ground.

At OCS: Your platoon rehearsal integrating all squads.

SUPPORT REHEARSAL

Specific function rehearsal—fires, sustainment, communications.

Example: Rehearse MEDEVAC procedures only.

Practice standard actions—react to contact, actions on OBJ, breach procedures.

Critical for muscle memory under stress.

If time permits, walk through the plan on the sand table with your teams—this is a basic combined arms rehearsal.

  • Full dress (actual ground, full kit)
  • Reduced force (key leaders only)
  • Terrain model (sand table)
  • Map (pointing at map/overlay)

1. What are the 8 TLP steps?

1. Receive the Mission • 2. Issue a Warning Order • 3. Make a Tentative Plan • 4. Initiate Movement • 5. Conduct Reconnaissance • 6. Complete the Plan • 7. Issue the Order • 8. Supervise and Refine

Mission • Enemy • Terrain & Weather • Troops Available • Time Available • Civil Considerations • (I)nformational Considerations

3. What is AGADAP?

COA development process: Analyze relative combat power • Generate options • Array forces • Develop concept of operations • Assign responsibilities • Prepare COA statement and sketch

4. What is a decisive point?

A geographic place, key event, or enabling system that, when acted upon, provides a marked advantage over the enemy. Types: Terrain-based, Time-based, Enemy-based.

Use no more than 1/3 of available time for your own planning. Give subordinates at least 2/3 of available time for their planning and preparation.
